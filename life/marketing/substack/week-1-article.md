# How to Follow the Spark Without Turning It Into School

One of the hardest things for parents is knowing what to do when a child gets genuinely interested in something.

Not mildly interested.
Not temporarily distracted.
Actually interested.

A lot of us have been trained to think learning only counts when it looks organized, measurable, or obviously academic. So when a child suddenly becomes fascinated by snakes, space, bugs, maps, storms, or desert trails, we can feel two opposite pulls at the same time.

One is the urge to let it happen.
The other is the urge to formalize it immediately.

Turn it into a unit.
Find a worksheet.
Make sure it becomes productive.
Make sure it "counts."

I understand that instinct. I really do.

I spent years as a teacher, and when you live around curriculum long enough, it becomes easy to assume that every meaningful learning moment has to be captured, labeled, and turned into a plan.

But some of the best learning I’ve seen never started that way.

It started with a question.
A detour.
A child noticing something before an adult decided it was important.

That’s one reason curiosity matters so much to me.

Recently I was thinking about a photo of Jonah and Noa standing by an "Outer Limits" trail sign in Sedona. Red rocks. Open desert. Big sky. The kind of place that makes kids ask things without even trying.

What kind of rock is that?
Why does the landscape look like this?
Who named this trail?
How far does it go?
What lives out here?

That moment holds a lot of what I want learning to feel like in our family.
Not forced.
Not detached from life.
Not trapped inside a system just because that system is familiar.

Alive.

That doesn’t mean I’m against structure.
It means I don’t want structure to crush the spark that made learning possible in the first place.

## The mistake a lot of us make

When a child gets curious, adults often do one of two things.

We either ignore it because it doesn’t fit the plan.
Or we overbuild around it so fast that the curiosity gets flattened.

A child says they’re interested in bugs, and within ten minutes we’re printing materials, assigning vocabulary, and creating a whole theme week.

Sometimes that can work.
But sometimes it turns a live question into one more managed activity.

Curiosity usually doesn’t need to be managed that aggressively.
It needs to be noticed.
Protected.
Extended just a little.

That extension can be simple.

You don’t need a perfect lesson.
You might just need:
- a library book
- a walk outside
- a sketchpad
- a field guide
- a conversation at lunch
- a few better questions
- or a quick ChatGPT prompt that helps you follow the thread

That’s it.

## What following the spark can actually look like

Let’s say your child gets interested in one bug they found outside.

You do not need to create a full insect curriculum by dinner.

You could:
- ask them to draw what they noticed
- look up its habitat
- compare it to another insect
- read one good page from a field guide
- ask ChatGPT for five kid-friendly questions about it
- go outside the next day and see if you can find another one

Now you’ve got observation, science, vocabulary, comparison, nature study, and memory working together.
But it still feels like life.

That matters.

Because when learning feels alive, kids tend to stay with it longer.
They ask better questions.
They remember more.
And parents feel less like they are dragging the whole experience uphill.

## Where AI fits in

I think AI is most useful here when it helps the parent respond without overcomplicating things.

It can help you:
- generate a few good questions
- explain a concept at your child’s level
- suggest a hands-on extension
- connect one interest to books, projects, or places
- reduce the mental load of planning in real time

That’s a good use of ChatGPT.

Not replacing thought.
Not keeping a child on a screen all day.
Not becoming the teacher.

Just helping you follow the spark while it’s still warm.

That’s a very different posture than using AI to automate school.

## A simple filter I come back to

When one of my kids gets interested in something, I try to ask:

What would help this curiosity grow one more step?

Not ten steps.
Not a complete system.
Just one more step.

That question changes a lot.
It keeps me from either dismissing the interest or smothering it with too much structure.

Sometimes one more step is a better question.
Sometimes it’s a book.
Sometimes it’s a short video, a map, a hike, a sketch, or a conversation in the car.

Sometimes it’s nothing more than saying,
"That’s interesting. Want to keep going with that?"

That small permission can matter more than we think.

## The kind of learning I want to protect

I want my kids to know that learning is bigger than school-shaped tasks.
I want them to feel that questions are worth following.
I want them to experience knowledge as something connected to the real world, not just something delivered for evaluation.

That’s part of what draws me to curiosity-driven learning.

It’s not about avoiding effort.
It’s about aiming effort in a direction that feels alive.

And I think a lot of families are hungry for that.
Not more pressure.
Not more educational performance.
Just a way of learning that feels more human, more flexible, and more connected to the life they actually want to live.

If that’s what you’re trying to build too, start smaller than you think.

The next time your child lights up around something, resist the urge to either ignore it or over-school it.
Ask what one more step might look like.

Then take it.
